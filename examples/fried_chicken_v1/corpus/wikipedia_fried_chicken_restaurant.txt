Fried chicken restaurant -- Wikipedia (paraphrased excerpts)

Definition

A fried chicken restaurant is a fast-food establishment whose
menu centres on Southern-style fried chicken -- pieces that are
battered or breaded and deep-fried. The category originated in
the United States and has since spread globally.

Major chains

The largest chain is KFC (formerly Kentucky Fried Chicken).
Other significant operators include Chick-fil-A, Popeyes
Louisiana Kitchen, Bojangles', Raising Cane's, and Church's
Texas Chicken. Regional US players such as Gus's World Famous
Fried Chicken and Zaxby's maintain strong local presences.
Canadian chains include Mary Brown's and Dixie Lee. The United
Kingdom hosts roughly 1,000 brands across ~1,700 outlets, with
particularly high density in inner-city areas. International
chains include Jollibee (Philippines), Bonchon (South Korea),
Chicken Licken (South Africa), Pollo Campero (Guatemala), and
Oporto (Australia).

Service model

Fried chicken restaurants operate predominantly as quick-service
restaurants. Most offer dine-in, takeaway, and third-party
delivery. Takeaway and delivery have historically accounted for
a large share of orders because fried chicken is a convenient
group food: buckets and family meals travel well in aggregate
quantity even if individual pieces lose some exterior texture.

Packaging

Typical takeaway packaging includes paper or foil-lined buckets
for larger orders, clamshell boxes for sandwiches, and folded
cardboard cartons or foil-backed bags for piece counts. All of
these are effectively closed containers: residual steam from the
hot breading is trapped against the crust during transport,
which softens the exterior. Chains vary in how aggressively they
vent their packaging -- some use louvered or perforated designs
intended to let steam escape, while others prioritise heat
retention.

Crispiness and holding

Industry notes on fried-chicken holding consistently identify
two texture degradation pathways once the food leaves the fryer:
moisture migration from the meat outward into the breading, and
steam condensation on the inside of closed packaging. Both
effects accumulate over the transport window (typically 10-30
minutes for takeaway, 20-45 minutes for delivery). Restoring
crispiness at home generally requires a brief reheat in a dry
high-heat environment such as an oven or air fryer; microwaves
accelerate the softening rather than reversing it.
