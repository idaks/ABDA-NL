This article examines the legal dispute over Shohei Ohtani's historic
50/50 home run ball through the lens of property law's "rule of
capture."

RULE OF CAPTURE

The foundational concept establishes that ownership of unowned things
belongs to whoever possesses them first. The article traces this back
to the seminal case Pierson v. Post, where a fox hunter's prey was
taken by a neighbor who ultimately "captured" it.

THE OHTANI BALL DISPUTE

On September 19, 2024, Ohtani achieved the first 50/50 season (home
runs and stolen bases). The resulting ball sold for $4.39 million, but
two individuals claimed ownership: Max Matus and Christian Zacek. Video
evidence showed both briefly controlled the ball during a scramble.

LEGAL COMPARISON FRAMEWORK

The article analyzes the case through two precedents:

- Like Pierson v. Post: Zacek's argument -- he secured final
  possession, completing the "hunt" Matus began.

- Like Popov v. Hayashi: Matus's argument -- he had temporary control
  before Zacek's interference removed it.

POPOV V. HAYASHI ANALYSIS

The article provides comprehensive coverage of the 2002 California
Superior Court decision involving competing claims over Barry Bonds's
record-breaking 73rd home run ball. It explains both sides' arguments:

- Popov's position: Temporary possession through control before mob
  interference. Where an individual undertakes significant steps to
  establish possession but is unlawfully prevented from doing so by
  others, they have a legally recognizable pre-possessory interest.

- Hayashi's counter: Popov never actually caught the ball; Hayashi
  recovered loose possession. Possession does not occur until the fan
  has complete control of the ball, as suggested by Professor Brian
  Gray's definition requiring that a person achieve complete control
  of the ball at the point in time that the momentum ceases.

The judge ordered an equitable 50/50 split and auction, establishing
important precedent for unclear ownership scenarios in rule of capture
doctrine.

PREDICTED OUTCOME FOR OHTANI BALL

The author suggests the court will likely follow Popov v. Hayashi,
which split ownership 50/50 when possession couldn't be definitively
established. Both parties' agreement to auction and split proceeds
($2.2 million each) supports this equitable resolution.

ACADEMIC SIGNIFICANCE

This case serves as a modern teaching tool that will probably "eclipse
Hayashi" as the primary example for demonstrating rule of capture
complexities in property law courses.
