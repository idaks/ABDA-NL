The Math Behind Tanking Just Keeps Getting Worse

Front-office strategy column (paraphrased)

Once a year, when some nominally competitive team decides in
February to "play the kids" and announce that they're done trying
to win, everyone writes the same column: here's what tanking
costs you, here's what it might get you, here's whether the
math works. We're going to do it one more time, because the math
has gotten meaningfully worse for tanking teams, and the
commentary hasn't caught up.

The pre-2019 lottery

For most of my adult life, tanking math was straightforward.
The worst team got a 25% shot at the top pick. The second-worst
got 19.9%. The margin for moving yourself from third-worst to
worst was real -- roughly nine percentage points of top-pick
probability, and a locked-in top-4 pick. If you were running a
rebuild, getting to the bottom of the standings was worth
something.

The 2019 flattening

The 2019 reform changed that math. The three worst records now
share 14% top-pick odds, identically. The fourth-worst drops to
12.5%. The odds curve is much flatter across the rest of the
lottery than it used to be. In expected-pick-position terms, the
tanking math flipped from a real gradient to something very
close to a flat line once you cross the bottom-3 threshold.

What this means in practice

Once you're in the bottom-3 band, the marginal value of your
last ten or fifteen losses is effectively zero. You're not
improving your expected pick. You're just losing.

Meanwhile, those last ten or fifteen losses are being absorbed
by your young players. Your 22-year-old is playing 35 minutes a
night in games that don't matter, in environments that reward
all the wrong habits, on a team that's been told the games
aren't serious. There is no public career-arc evidence that this
helps him become a better version of himself.

When tanking still makes sense

Full tanking -- sit the vets, minutes-cap the stars, run lineups
designed to lose -- can still make sense for teams with:

- No young core worth protecting.
- A roster full of expiring veterans where you'd prefer draft
  capital.
- A team budget that makes paying luxury tax untenable for a
  team that isn't contending.

In those cases, the 2019 reform doesn't change the basic logic.
You're not choosing between a number-one pick and a number-
three pick; you're choosing between lottery participation and
the play-in. That choice still favors the lottery.

When it doesn't

For teams with a top-5 young core already on the roster, the
math has changed decisively. Compete hard, accept the pick
position you end up with, keep your young players in
environments that build good habits. You are not going to catch
up in the lottery by tanking. You might give back years of
development.

The biggest front-office mistake of the next five years will be
a team that tanks because that's what it "looks like" rebuilding
teams do, ignoring that the rules changed and their roster is no
longer the kind where tanking is the right move.
