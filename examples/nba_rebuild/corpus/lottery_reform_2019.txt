NBA Draft Lottery Reform (2019)
Paraphrased public explanation

Background

Prior to 2019 the NBA draft lottery weighted odds steeply toward
the worst record in the league. The worst team had a 25.0%
probability of the #1 overall pick; the second-worst 19.9%; the
third-worst 15.6%; and so on. In practice this structure rewarded
the late-season losing streaks that came to be known as "tanking."

2019 Reform

Following league and owner discussion about competitive integrity,
the lottery odds were flattened beginning with the 2019 draft:

- The three worst teams now share identical 14.0% odds at the
  #1 overall pick.
- The odds curve falls more gradually through the rest of the
  lottery, so positions 4 through 14 have less steeply weighted
  expected returns.
- The first four picks are determined by the lottery; positions
  5-14 follow in reverse order of standings.

Effect on the Tanking Argument

The classic tanking calculation -- "guarantee yourself a top-3
chance by having the league's worst record" -- no longer holds.
Once a team is in the bottom-3 band, further losing does not
improve its #1-pick odds. The expected pick-position gain from
moving from third-worst to worst is now much smaller than it was
pre-reform.

Publicly stated league objective: reduce the incentive to pursue
season-long losing while preserving a lottery mechanism for
competitive-balance rebuilding.

Analyst Consensus

Most public draft-strategy analysis treats the 2019 reform as
having materially weakened, though not eliminated, the tanking
incentive. The pick-value argument survives in the sense that
lottery participation still beats a playoff floor; it has been
substantially weakened in the sense that the marginal value of
the last 10-15 losses is close to zero.

(Source: paraphrased from public NBA announcements and widely
reported draft-odds analyses. Illustrative only; see the official
NBA rulebook for exact lottery procedures.)
