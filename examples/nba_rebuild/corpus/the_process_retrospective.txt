The Process: Ten Years Later

Retrospective analysis (paraphrased)

The Philadelphia 76ers' "Process" rebuild, running roughly from
2013 through 2017, is the most visible explicit tanking project
in recent NBA history, and the one most often cited in strategy
debates. Ten years on, its outcome is worth reviewing carefully
rather than caricatured in either direction.

What was done

Under general manager Sam Hinkie, the 76ers committed to a
multi-year stripping of the roster. Veteran assets were traded
for picks and future considerations. Competitive incentives in
individual seasons were de-emphasized in favor of draft-asset
accumulation. The public framing was that the expected value of
a draft-heavy rebuild exceeded that of any other path available
to a team that lacked a franchise cornerstone.

What it cost

Four consecutive seasons of well below .500 basketball. Attendance
and television metrics declined. The team drafted high every
year, but high picks came with their own costs: injury
uncertainty (Joel Embiid missed his first two seasons), player
development risk (Jahlil Okafor, Michael Carter-Williams, and
Markelle Fultz did not pan out as core pieces), and league-wide
reputational damage that led to the 2017 lottery reform and
helped motivate the 2019 flattening.

What it produced

The 76ers did acquire Joel Embiid in the 2014 draft and Ben
Simmons in the 2016 draft, both central to the team's
competitive window in the late 2010s. They also drafted Dario
Saric, Robert Covington, and accumulated first-round assets that
were later converted into roster pieces through trades. From
2017 to 2022, the team made the playoffs every year, with
several second-round exits but no conference-finals appearances.

How to read the record

Defenders of the Process point to Embiid as sufficient
justification: a franchise cornerstone is a rare asset, and the
rebuild produced one. Critics point to the long period of
non-competitive basketball, the damage to the league brand, and
the counterfactual question of whether a less extreme rebuild
could have produced a similar cornerstone.

Perhaps most importantly for the current strategic debate: the
Process was undertaken under the pre-2019 lottery odds. The
top-pick gradient that made bottom-3 finishes valuable no longer
exists. A franchise that attempted a Process-style rebuild today
would do so at higher cost (three guaranteed bottom-3 finishes
no longer secures three 25% top-pick shots) and lower expected
return (the marginal value of being the worst team rather than
the third-worst is now zero).

Anti-tanking reforms have been substantially motivated by the
league's explicit desire to prevent another Process. The 2019
flattening and the 2023 second apron are, in part, structural
responses to the possibility of another team adopting the same
strategy.

Implication for current strategy

The Process is a cautionary tale precisely because it was a
rational response to the rules as they were, and the rules have
since been changed specifically to make it less rational. Teams
that invoke the Process framing today are reasoning from a rule
set that no longer exists. For franchises with a young core
already in place, the asymmetric risk now runs the other way:
the downside of sabotaging development is structurally larger
than the upside of modestly improved lottery odds.
