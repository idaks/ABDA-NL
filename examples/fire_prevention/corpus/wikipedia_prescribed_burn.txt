Controlled burn -- Wikipedia (paraphrased excerpts)

A controlled burn, also called prescribed burn or prescribed fire,
is the deliberate application of fire to a vegetated landscape
under specified environmental and operational conditions to
achieve land-management objectives. Controlled burns are used to
reduce accumulated fuel loads, restore fire-adapted ecosystems,
maintain specific habitat conditions, and achieve cultural or
agricultural purposes.

History

Indigenous peoples across North America, Australia, and other
fire-adapted landscapes have cultivated ecosystems with fire for
thousands of years. Cultural burning practices shaped forest
structure, grassland composition, and wildlife habitat in many
regions prior to European colonization.

Federal and state fire-suppression policy in the twentieth
century -- exemplified by the U.S. "10 AM policy" of the 1930s
and analogous approaches elsewhere -- removed fire from many of
these landscapes. The cumulative effect has been a significant
transformation of forest structure, with consequences that
contemporary fire science increasingly links to the severity of
recent wildfires.

Ecological objectives

In dry mixed-conifer and ponderosa-pine forests, prescribed fire
is used to reduce surface and ladder fuels, maintain open forest
conditions, promote regeneration of fire-adapted species, and
reduce the probability of high-severity crown fire during
subsequent wildfires. Treatment effects have been studied
extensively; tree mortality in post-treatment wildfire events is
typically 30-70% lower than in untreated matched controls.

Operational planning

Prescribed burns require a formal burn plan specifying objectives,
fuel and weather prescriptions, ignition patterns, smoke
management, resource staging, and contingency procedures. Go /
no-go criteria are evaluated on the day of the burn.

Smoke management and air quality

Smoke from prescribed burns affects air quality downwind of the
burn unit. In the United States, smoke management is addressed
through Smoke Management Programs under State Implementation
Plans. Most state programs include burn-day PM2.5 forecasts at
populated-area receptors, with a go / no-go criterion tied to the
federal National Ambient Air Quality Standards (NAAQS).

Tradeoffs

The short-term air-quality impacts of prescribed burns are often
compared against the likely long-term air-quality impacts of the
wildfires that prescribed burning is intended to mitigate.
High-severity wildfires in fuel-rich forests produce substantially
larger smoke emissions, over longer periods, with less control
over exposure than prescribed burns conducted under favorable
conditions. The accumulating consensus in fire science is that
reintroducing fire through carefully planned prescribed burns and
managed wildfires is preferable to continued exclusion.

Cultural burning and contemporary partnerships

Growing recognition of Indigenous fire stewardship has led to
formal partnerships between federal and state land-management
agencies and Tribal Nations in many regions. These partnerships
range from consultation on burn planning to tribal leadership of
prescribed-fire operations on homelands and adjacent public
lands.
