EPA PM2.5 Air-Quality Standards and Prescribed Fire
Paraphrased regulatory summary

National Ambient Air Quality Standards (NAAQS)

Under the Clean Air Act, the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency
sets National Ambient Air Quality Standards for six criteria
pollutants. For fine particulate matter (PM2.5), the current 24-hour
standard is 35 micrograms per cubic meter (annual 9 micrograms per
cubic meter as of the 2024 revision).

Prescribed Fire Under the SIP Framework

States implement NAAQS through State Implementation Plans (SIPs).
Prescribed fire is addressed via Smoke Management Programs (SMPs)
that specify:

- Required burn-day smoke-dispersion forecasts.
- Notification and coordination requirements with air-quality
  authorities.
- Burn-day go/no-go criteria keyed to predicted 24-hour PM2.5 at
  specified receptor locations.

Forecast Exceedance

Under most state SMPs, if the burn-day dispersion forecast
predicts 24-hour PM2.5 exceedance of the NAAQS threshold at a
populated-area receptor, the burn permit is withheld for that day
regardless of ecological or programmatic justification. This is a
categorical constraint, not a balancing test. Managers may
reschedule the burn to a day with a more favorable forecast.

Distinguishing the Strict Constraint from Balancing

The PM2.5 exceedance rule is an example of a regulatory constraint
that is not subject to trade-off: it is not that the ecological
benefit must be sufficiently large to justify a near-exceedance
day; it is that exceedance days are off-limits regardless. For
formal modeling, this is captured as a strict rule, distinct from
the balancing that characterizes most other burn-day judgments.
