Tribal Fire Stewardship: Background and Policy Position
Paraphrased public statement

Overview

Indigenous communities across western North America have
cultivated landscapes with fire for millennia. Cultural burning
(also termed "good fire" or "beneficial fire" in tribal usage)
served multiple purposes: food and fiber cultivation, wildlife
habitat management, ceremonial renewal, and the maintenance of
open, park-like forest conditions that reduced the severity of
lightning-caused wildfires.

Federal and state fire-exclusion policies of the 20th century
displaced these practices. The cumulative effect has been a
transformation of forest structure and an increase in the severity
of contemporary wildfires -- an outcome that growing scientific
consensus now attributes in part to the absence of the fire
regimes that Indigenous stewardship historically maintained.

Policy Position

The reintroduction of cultural fire, led by and coordinated with
Tribal Nations, is a priority for forest restoration in homelands
across the region. Partnerships between tribes, federal land-
management agencies, and state air authorities are increasingly
recognized as the appropriate framework for burn planning on and
adjacent to tribal lands.

Key points commonly asserted in tribal position statements:

- Cultural fire is not the same as federally defined prescribed
  fire; it reflects specific site-level relationships and
  objectives that differ from fuel-treatment-only framings.

- Documented historical burning regimes constitute substantive
  evidence about what the land and its biotic communities evolved
  with, and should inform contemporary fire planning.

- Short-term air-quality considerations are real but must be
  weighed against the long-term air-quality and cultural costs of
  catastrophic wildfires produced by continuing fire exclusion.

Implication for This Decision

Where a unit has a documented cultural fire regime, there is
substantive justification for burning grounded in both ecological
and cultural evidence. The presence of such a regime is not merely
one consideration among many; it reflects a time-tested land-care
practice that complementary fire-ecology science has converged on.

(Source: paraphrased from public tribal fire-stewardship position
statements and the Indigenous Peoples Burning Network. Illustrative
only.)
