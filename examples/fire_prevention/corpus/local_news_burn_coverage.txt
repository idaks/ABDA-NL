Forest Service Proposes Prescribed Burn in Contested Airshed;
Residents Divided

Environment desk (Spring, paraphrased)

The U.S. Forest Service announced plans this week to conduct a
prescribed burn on a 1,800-acre forest unit bordering a rural
community in the southern portion of the region, drawing a mix
of support and opposition at a public meeting held at the town
hall.

Agency fire planners described the unit as overdue for treatment.
"Fuel loading is well above what this forest evolved with," the
district's fuels specialist said. "If we don't treat this unit
with fire under conditions we choose, it's eventually going to
burn under conditions we don't."

Several community members raised concerns about smoke exposure.
A retired nurse cited the community's aging population, elevated
rates of asthma, and the location of the local school within the
predicted smoke-drift corridor. "We support forest health. We
just need the Agency to be honest about what we're breathing."

Tribal representatives from the nearest federally recognized
tribe spoke in support of the burn. A tribal fire steward
described the unit as part of a cultural-use area where burning
had been conducted for generations prior to federal fire
exclusion. "This is good fire. The land has been asking for it."

The district ranger described the burn-day protocol in detail.
The Agency's smoke-management plan requires a PM2.5 forecast at
the school-district air-quality monitor the morning of the burn;
if the forecast predicts exceedance of the federal 24-hour NAAQS
threshold, the burn is postponed regardless of operational
readiness. "Community exposure to PM2.5 above the federal
standard is not a trade-off we're allowed to make," he said.

Questions persisted about whether mechanical-thinning
alternatives had been adequately considered. The Agency
committed to publishing the alternatives analysis as part of the
decision memo.

A final decision and tentative burn window are expected within
sixty days, pending completion of environmental review and
coordination with the state air-quality authority.
