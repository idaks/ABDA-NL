Regional Fire Situation Report

Season outlook

Seasonal outlook products integrate drought status, snowpack,
long-term precipitation anomalies, and vegetation-moisture
observations to forecast fire-season conditions. The current
regional outlook indicates above-normal significant wildfire
potential across much of the subject area, with dry-conifer
forests at heightened risk due to:

- Fuel loading above historical norms, driven by a century of
  fire exclusion.
- Drought-stressed vegetation following several consecutive
  below-normal precipitation years.
- Projected warmer-than-normal temperatures during the core
  fire months.

Fuel-treatment priorities

Prescribed burning and mechanical thinning followed by
prescribed burning remain the primary tools for reducing
large-fire risk in dry-forest fuel types. Agency planning
prioritizes treatments:

- In the Wildland-Urban Interface (WUI) where values at risk are
  high.
- On forest units with extended treatment intervals (>15 years
  since last treatment) where ecological benefit is greatest.
- Where tribal and community partnerships are in place to
  coordinate operations and stewardship objectives.

Air-quality coordination

All prescribed-burn plans are coordinated through the state Smoke
Management Program. Burn-day PM2.5 forecasts are evaluated against
the NAAQS threshold at designated receptor locations prior to
final go / no-go determinations. In periods of sensitive public-
health conditions, permit issuance may be constrained.

Cultural burning partnerships

Several tribes in the region have entered into formal
co-stewardship agreements with federal land-management agencies.
These agreements formalize tribal leadership roles in burn
planning and execution on ancestral lands and adjacent public
lands, and prioritize cultural as well as fuel-reduction
objectives.

Key message

The net fire-season risk picture supports accelerating treatment
where feasible, subject to burn-day air-quality coordination and
operational-safety constraints. Continued fire exclusion at
historical levels is not compatible with current fuel and climate
conditions in the dry-forest landscapes of the region.
