Proton-pump inhibitor -- Wikipedia (paraphrased excerpts)

Proton-pump inhibitors (PPIs) are a class of medications that
reduce stomach acid production by irreversibly inhibiting the
hydrogen/potassium ATPase proton pump on the luminal surface of
gastric parietal cells. PPIs are among the most widely prescribed
medications in the world.

Members of the class

Commonly prescribed PPIs include:
- Omeprazole (Prilosec)
- Esomeprazole (Nexium)
- Lansoprazole (Prevacid)
- Pantoprazole (Protonix)
- Rabeprazole (Aciphex)
- Dexlansoprazole (Dexilant)

Members differ in CYP450 metabolism and drug-interaction profiles.
Omeprazole and esomeprazole are notable CYP2C19 inhibitors;
pantoprazole has comparatively minimal CYP2C19 activity.

Clinical uses

PPIs are first-line therapy for gastroesophageal reflux disease
(GERD), peptic ulcer disease, Barrett's esophagus surveillance,
Zollinger-Ellison syndrome, and stress-ulcer prophylaxis in
critically ill patients. They are also used as part of eradication
regimens for Helicobacter pylori infection.

Adverse effects and safety considerations

Long-term use has been associated with a range of potential
adverse outcomes, though causal relationships are not always
established. Reported associations include:

- Fracture risk: modestly increased risk of hip, wrist, and spine
  fracture in observational studies of long-term users, with
  hazard ratios generally in the 1.2-1.4 range.
- Bone mineral density: the mechanism is debated; hypotheses
  include reduced calcium absorption and osteoclast modulation.
- Clostridioides difficile infection: small absolute increase in
  risk.
- Community-acquired pneumonia: inconsistent evidence.
- Clopidogrel interaction: omeprazole and esomeprazole reduce the
  conversion of clopidogrel to its active metabolite via CYP2C19
  inhibition. The clinical significance has been debated; the
  COGENT trial (2010) did not detect excess ischemic events with
  pantoprazole, but pharmacodynamic studies show clear
  differences between agents.
- Chronic kidney disease: observational associations reported;
  mechanism uncertain.

Formulary and deprescribing considerations

Many institutions maintain formal deprescribing protocols for
long-term PPI users without a clear ongoing indication. Patients
with compelling indications (Barrett's esophagus, recurrent
bleeding ulcer history, severe erosive esophagitis, Zollinger-
Ellison syndrome) generally remain on chronic therapy. Patient-
specific interactions -- including antiplatelet therapy,
osteoporosis risk, and polypharmacy considerations -- are
commonly weighed by pharmacy and therapeutics committees.
