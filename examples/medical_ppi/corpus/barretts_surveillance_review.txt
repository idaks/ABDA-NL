Barrett's Esophagus: Surveillance and Chemoprevention
Narrative review -- paraphrased synthesis

Background

Barrett's esophagus (BE) is a metaplastic condition in which the
distal esophagus develops intestinalized columnar epithelium,
typically as a consequence of chronic gastroesophageal reflux
disease. BE is the principal precursor lesion for esophageal
adenocarcinoma (EAC), a cancer with poor survival at late stages.

Progression Rates

Published progression rates vary by dysplasia grade:

- Non-dysplastic BE: approximately 0.1-0.3% per patient-year
  progression to EAC.
- Low-grade dysplasia: approximately 0.5-1.0% per patient-year.
- High-grade dysplasia: substantially higher; most major centers
  recommend endoscopic eradication therapy.

Chemopreventive Role of Acid Suppression

Long-term PPI therapy reduces esophageal acid exposure and, through
multiple proposed mechanisms, may reduce the rate of progression
to dysplasia and cancer. The AspECT trial (Jankowski et al., Lancet
2018) randomized BE patients to high-dose vs. low-dose esomeprazole
with or without aspirin and reported a modest composite benefit
for high-dose esomeprazole combined with aspirin over a median 8.9
years of follow-up.

Observational evidence has generally supported an inverse
relationship between PPI adherence and BE progression, although
residual confounding by indication is difficult to rule out.

Guideline Consensus

Major GI societies (AGA, ACG, BSG) converge on a recommendation
for long-term PPI therapy in BE patients as part of a broader
surveillance strategy that includes endoscopic screening at
intervals determined by dysplasia status.

Interaction with Other Risks

The chemopreventive rationale for PPI therapy in BE does not
override patient-specific risks. The contemporary consensus is
that PPI therapy should be maintained unless a specific competing
risk (clinically significant drug interaction, major contribution
to fracture risk in a fragile patient) outweighs the cancer-
reduction benefit. The threshold for that judgment varies among
clinicians and institutions.
