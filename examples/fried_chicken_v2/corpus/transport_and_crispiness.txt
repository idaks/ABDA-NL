Short guide: why takeaway fried chicken loses crispiness

Starting condition

Fried chicken leaves the fryer with a crust whose surface has
been dehydrated by contact with hot oil. The crust's crispiness
is a function of how dry that outer layer is and how porous the
breading has become during frying.

Transport environment

Takeaway fried chicken is placed, still hot, into a closed
container -- a box, a bucket, a clamshell, or a paper bag lined
with foil. Inside that container:

1. The meat continues to release water vapour as it cools.
2. The air inside the container quickly reaches a high relative
   humidity because the container is sealed or near-sealed.
3. Water vapour condenses on the inside of the lid and on the
   breading itself, which is cooler than the meat.
4. The breading reabsorbs moisture from the saturated air and
   from direct contact with condensate on the box surface.

Result

By the time the chicken is unboxed (typically 10-30 minutes
after leaving the restaurant), the crust has softened
measurably. The degree of softening is proportional to the
transport duration and to how tightly sealed the packaging is.
This softening is mechanical and chemical, not bacterial -- the
food is still safe to eat; it is simply no longer crispy.

Restoration options

A short reheat in a dry, high-temperature environment reverses
most of this moisture reabsorption. The standard options are:

- Air fryer: 3-5 minutes at 180-200 C. Effective because the
  high-velocity hot air strips the reabsorbed moisture from the
  breading surface.
- Conventional oven: 8-12 minutes at 200 C on a wire rack.
  Effective but slow to preheat.
- Microwave: not recommended. Microwaving the chicken heats the
  water in the breading and drives it further into the crust,
  making the softening worse.

Implication for the ordering decision

If a suitable reheating device is available at home, ordering
to-go is roughly equivalent to dining in from a texture
perspective. If no reheating device is available, to-go
strictly loses on crispiness.
